# What is Pink Noise?
Pink noise is characterised by its 1/f frequency response (and is thus sometimes referred to in literature as '1/f noise')[^2]. Where white noise has a flat frequency response across all frequencies in the sampling range, Pink noise has a sloped frequency response at -10dB per decade[^2]. The figure below shows an FFT of a white and pink noise sample to demonstrate this.

Pink noise is of particular interest to the scientific community due to its prevalence in various natural phenomena [^1]. For anyone wanting to learn more about it there's a plethora of material out there for further reading.

With regards to the generation of pink noise, there has been a lot of discussion with how best to achieve this [^2][^3][^4][^5]. Most of the techniques I've encountered are some variation of filtering a white noise signal with a "pinking" filter that has a frequency response of roughly -10dB per decade. There is also algorithmic methods of producing pink noise, one of which will be discussed in this article.
